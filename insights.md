Project Insights: What the Data Tells Us
We analyzed over 450,000 delivery records to find out where orders get delayed or cancelled. While riders drive efficiently once they have the food, the data shows two major problems happening before they start the delivery.

1. Drivers spend too much time waiting at restaurants
Our data shows that once a driver picks up the food, it takes about 15 to 18 minutes to deliver it to the customer. This is standard and highly efficient for city traffic.

However, when we looked at the phase before that—the time between a driver accepting an order and actually picking up the food—we noticed a major bottleneck:

Short-distance orders (under 1 km away): It takes 13.22 minutes on average for the driver to get the food.

Medium-distance orders (1 to 3 km away): It takes 14.58 minutes on average.

What this means (and why it happens):
It is completely normal for a restaurant to need 10 to 15 minutes to cook fresh food, like a burger or paneer masala. The bottleneck here isn't the kitchen staff's fault.

The app pings and sends a driver the exact same second the restaurant gets the cooking ticket. Because the driver is usually less than 1 km away, they arrive at the restaurant in just 2 to 3 minutes approx. As a result, drivers are forced to stand around at the counter for 10 to 11 minutes doing nothing while waiting for the food to finish cooking. Since 98% of all orders fall into these short and medium-distance categories, this timing mismatch may be the bottleneck on the platform.

2. The 58-Minute "Dead Zone" for Cancelled Orders
When an order cannot be completed (for example, if a kitchen is completely overwhelmed and has to cancel), the app takes way too long to realize something went wrong.

For short-distance orders, it takes an average of 58.54 minutes from the moment a driver accepts the order to the moment it officially gets cancelled.
For medium-distance orders, it takes 56.90 minutes.

What this means:
Instead of identifying a broken order within 15 minutes, the system lets nearly an hour pass by before triggering a cancellation.
This creates a triple-loss scenario:
Wasted Driver Earnings: A driver is trapped at a restaurant for an hour earning zero money, causing frustration and platform dissatisfaction.
Bad Customer Experience: The customer is left waiting for an hour, only to be told their meal isn't coming.
System Capacity Drops: Because drivers are trapped waiting on doomed orders, there are fewer active drivers available to deliver healthy orders.

Simple, Operational Solutions
To fix these issues, the platform does not need restaurants to cook faster; it just needs to schedule smarter.
Implement Delayed Dispatching: If a dish takes 12 minutes to prepare and a driver is only 3 minutes away, the app should wait 9 minutes before sending the notification to the driver. This gives the kitchen plenty of time to cook fresh food, while keeping the driver free to complete other deliveries in the meantime.

Create a 20-Minute Safety Alert: If a driver has been stationary at a restaurant for more than 20 minutes without a recorded pickup, the system should automatically flag the order. This allows customer support to intervene, reassign, or cancel the order early instead of letting it sit for an hour.

Rider Experience vs. Delivery Speed
We ran a custom analysis to see if a rider's experience level impacts how fast an order gets delivered. We grouped riders by their experience—New Riders (under 10 lifetime trips) versus Experienced Riders ( veteran drivers)—and split their performance into pure driving time versus total trip time.

The data revealed a massive performance gap:

New Riders: Take 28.57 minutes to drive the food to the customer, and 44.22 minutes for the total trip.

Experienced Riders: Take 16.23 minutes to drive the food, and 30.22 minutes for the total trip.

What this means:
The Navigation Advantage: Veteran drivers are 12.34 minutes faster just during the physical driving and drop-off phase. They clearly know city shortcuts better, navigate traffic efficiently, and lose less time finding parking or locating tricky apartment/office addresses.

The Restaurant Handoff Advantage: Experienced riders save an extra 1.66 minutes at the restaurant counter. Because they are familiar with the layouts and restaurant staff, they get the food handed to them faster than a new driver.

The Hidden Platform Risk: Experienced drivers handle over 99% of all deliveries on the platform (442,307 orders vs. only 2,475 from new riders). While this keeps the current network highly efficient, it highlights a major business risk: if the platform experiences high driver churn and loses these veterans, overall delivery times across the city will skyrocket.

Strategic Recommendation:
Gamified Onboarding & Routing Help: New riders need extra assistance during their first 10–20 trips. The app should provide them with high-accuracy internal mall maps for restaurant pickups and specific gate-entry notes for large apartment complexes to help close that 12-minute navigation gap faster.
